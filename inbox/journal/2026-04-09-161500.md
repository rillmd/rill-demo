Deep work afternoon on the context window benchmark. Good day.

The "hot / warm / cold" tiering I sketched a few weeks ago actually holds up empirically. Quick recap of the tiers for my own sanity: hot = in-prompt (fastest, most expensive per token), warm = in-session cache (fast, amortized), cold = retrieved from the vector store on demand (slow, cheap).

Ran a batch of evals today against a fixed set of 40 questions. Cold-tier retrieval is about 3x slower end-to-end than warm, which I expected. What surprised me: **quality barely moved**. As long as the retriever surfaces the right 2-3 chunks, the model does fine. The quality gap I thought existed between "fully in context" and "retrieved" was mostly my imagination.

The real lever is retrieval quality, not context placement. That's a meaningful reframe — it means we should be investing in better chunking and better embeddings, not in bigger context windows.

One caveat: this is on a well-structured corpus. I'd bet the story is different for unstructured free-form text where retrieval has less to grab onto. Want to try the same benchmark on meeting transcripts next week as a stress test.

Going to write this up properly over the weekend. Feels like it matters.

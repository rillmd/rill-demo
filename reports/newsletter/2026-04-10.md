# Week of April 6-10, 2026

A week of unusually clean wins. Three different threads advanced materially, and one long-running decision finally landed. Writing this up partly to mark the progress and partly to make sure next week starts with clear priorities rather than momentum alone.

## This Week in API v2

The UUID v7 decision is done. After two weeks of circling the ID generation question, Alex's comparison document made the choice unambiguous: UUID v7 wins on k-sortability, native database support is landing soon in our primary store, and the library ecosystem is healthy enough for production use. The decision was announced Tuesday morning, and the first database schema migration PR went into review Thursday. Rollback procedures are documented. The path from here to a migrated v2 schema is the clearest it's been since we started.

The typed error schema spec closed out this week at v1.0. The final structure uses five categories (validation, authentication, authorization, resource, system) with retryable semantics defined at the category level, which eliminates the per-error ambiguity that was slowing down the earlier drafts. Jordan flagged a good concern about how client SDKs should surface retryable errors without leaking the internal taxonomy, which got resolved with a thin wrapper type. Ready for formal review next week.

## Experiments and Side Projects

The async standup trial completed its first week, and the signal is clearer than I expected. The team ran daily written updates instead of a 15-minute live standup, and the results were positive across the board: lower total time cost, higher information retention, and a channel archive that's already proving useful as a reference artifact. In today's review meeting we agreed to move to a hybrid model: daily async updates plus a single Monday sync focused specifically on blocker resolution. This preserves what verbal standups are actually good at (live back-and-forth on tangled issues) while eliminating what they're bad at (repeating information that reads better written down).

## Side Project: LLM Eval Harness

The LLM eval harness side project reached a v0 milestone: it runs end to end, with a clean split between dataset loader, model runner, and judge. The architecture was informed by the LLM-as-judge literature from the previous weekend's reading, and early measurements are already producing interesting results on the hot/warm/cold context tiering hypothesis. The harness is rough -- no result persistence, synchronous execution -- but it's iterable, which is what matters at this stage.

## Technical Reading

Two pieces from this week that influenced how I'm thinking:

- An article on LLM-as-judge evaluation patterns that shaped the architecture of the eval harness side project. The key insight: separating judge prompts from dataset construction makes iteration dramatically faster.
- A piece on incremental API migration strategies that reinforced the direction we'd already chosen for v2. The strategies it described for dual-writing during transitions may be directly applicable to our schema migration.

## Team Notes
